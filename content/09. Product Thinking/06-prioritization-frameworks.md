# Prioritization Frameworks

## Executive Summary
Prioritization frameworks help teams make explicit, defensible decisions about what to build next. In TPM interviews, you are expected to choose and apply frameworks based on context — not recite definitions.

Strong answers show you can run a prioritization process under competing stakeholder pressures.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Prioritization is one of the most politically charged and highest-impact TPM activities.
- Architecture relevance: Technical debt and platform investment compete directly with features.
- Interview relevance: Frequent practical scenario question.

## Core Concepts
### Common Frameworks
- RICE: Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort.
- ICE: Impact × Confidence × Ease.
- MoSCoW: Must Have / Should Have / Could Have / Won't Have.
- Kano Model: Basic needs vs performance vs delight features.
- Weighted scoring: Custom criteria with stakeholder-agreed weights.

### Internal Working
Criteria defined -> items scored -> ranked list produced -> decisions made with explicit rationale.

### ASCII Diagram
Item Backlog -> Scoring Criteria -> Weighted Rank -> Decision + Rationale

### Trade-offs
- Quantitative scoring feels objective but depends heavily on input quality.
- Qualitative frameworks are faster but harder to defend under pressure.

## Real-world Example
A platform team used RICE to compare technical debt remediation against new feature requests. By quantifying reach and effort, they showed the debt work had higher ROI — earning executive buy-in for allocation.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- Are scoring criteria agreed with all stakeholders before scoring starts?
- How is technical debt and reliability work represented in prioritization?
- What happens to good ideas that are de-prioritized?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I choose prioritization framework by context — RICE for quantitative cross-team alignment, MoSCoW for fast scoping under time pressure. I make criteria explicit and pre-agreed so the process produces decisions, not debates."

## Common Mistakes
- Scoring after decisions are made to justify predetermined choices.
- No representation of technical health in prioritization.
- Using a single framework dogmatically.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Framework choice depends on context and stakeholder dynamics.
- Pre-agree criteria before scoring.
- Always show technical work alongside product features.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Walk me through how you would prioritize a backlog of 20 items.
2. How do you handle stakeholders who disagree with prioritization results?
3. How do you represent technical debt in product prioritization?
4. When is RICE the wrong framework?
5. How do you keep prioritization decisions transparent?

## Key Takeaways
- No single framework works everywhere.
- Process legitimacy matters as much as the output.
- TPMs should lead prioritization as a structured, inclusive process.
