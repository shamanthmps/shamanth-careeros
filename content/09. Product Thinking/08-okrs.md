# OKRs

## Executive Summary
OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a goal-setting framework aligning teams to outcomes rather than activities. In interviews, you are expected to write strong OKRs, identify bad ones, and explain how to use OKRs to drive accountability.

Strong answers show that OKRs are tools for focus and learning, not performance theater.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: OKRs define what success looks like for programs.
- Architecture relevance: Technical objectives should be visible in company or team OKR structures.
- Interview relevance: Direct topic and embedded in many behavioral questions.

## Core Concepts
### Definitions
- Objective: Qualitative, inspiring statement of what to achieve.
- Key Result: Measurable outcome that indicates progress toward the objective.
- Stretch OKR: Ambitious target expecting 70% achievement.
- Committed OKR: Must-achieve operational target.

### Internal Working
Company OKRs -> team OKRs aligned -> quarterly check-ins -> retrospective and reset.

### ASCII Diagram
Objective (what) -> Key Results (how measured) -> Initiatives (how delivered)

### Trade-offs
- Ambitious stretch targets drive innovation but risk gaming metrics.
- Conservative targets produce predictability but suppress ambition.

## Real-world Example
A team changed their OKR from "Launch notification system" to "Reduce user time to first action by 25% via better onboarding notifications." The second version tied the work to a measurable customer outcome rather than a delivery milestone.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- Do key results measure outcomes or outputs?
- Are OKRs reviewed and discussed regularly or just written quarterly?
- What happens when a key result becomes impossible mid-quarter?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I write OKRs that measure business and customer outcomes, not delivery milestones. I use check-ins to update confidence levels and catch early when assumptions are wrong, treating missed OKRs as learning signals rather than failures."

## Common Mistakes
- Writing key results as tasks (e.g., "Launch X feature").
- No mid-quarter review cadence.
- Tying OKRs directly to performance ratings — kills honest grading.

## 5-Minute Revision
- OKR = aspirational objective + measurable outcome.
- Key results should be metrics, not milestones.
- Check-ins make OKRs useful, not just annual.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Write an OKR for improving API reliability.
2. How do you handle a key result that becomes irrelevant mid-quarter?
3. How do you align team OKRs to company OKRs?
4. What makes an OKR "too easy" or "too hard"?
5. How do you prevent OKR gaming?

## Key Takeaways
- Good OKRs measure outcomes, not outputs.
- Regular check-ins are what make OKRs work.
- TPMs should drive OKR clarity and honest retrospectives.
