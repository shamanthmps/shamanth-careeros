# TPM + Product Collaboration

## Executive Summary
The TPM-PM relationship is one of the most important in product engineering organizations. Interviewers evaluate whether TPMs can complement PMs without overlapping, challenging without obstructing, and add execution depth without taking ownership of product decisions.

Strong answers show a mature partnership model with clear accountability boundaries.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Healthy TPM-PM collaboration directly increases program velocity and quality.
- Architecture relevance: Technical feasibility inputs to product decisions require TPM leadership.
- Interview relevance: Frequently asked behavioral and leadership question.

## Core Concepts
### Role Clarity
- PM owns: what to build and why (product strategy, prioritization, user research).
- TPM owns: how to deliver it safely (scope, dependencies, risk, execution).
- Shared: trade-off discussions, launch readiness, success measurement.

### Internal Working
PM defines outcome -> TPM translates to execution plan -> collaborative trade-off resolution -> aligned delivery.

### ASCII Diagram
PM (what/why) <-> TPM (how/when/risk) -> Engineering (build) -> Users (outcome)

### Trade-offs
- Too much TPM overlap with PM reduces PM accountability.
- Too little engagement leaves execution-product gap.

## Real-world Example
A TPM and PM had overlapping scope debates that slowed decision-making. They defined a clear DACI: PM is Decider on product scope, TPM is Decider on technical scope and timeline, both are Approvers on launch gates. Velocity improved.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- Where is the accountability boundary between PM and TPM on this program?
- Is the PM getting technical feasibility input early enough?
- Are we aligned on success metrics before starting delivery?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I position myself as the PM's execution partner: I own translating product goals into technical plans, surfacing risks and trade-offs early, and holding the delivery accountable. I never make product decisions unilaterally but I always bring execution perspective to product decisions."

## Common Mistakes
- TPM acting as a shadow PM.
- TPM deferring all product questions without adding technical perspective.
- No defined accountability model between roles.

## 5-Minute Revision
- PM owns what and why; TPM owns how and when.
- Collaboration friction usually means unclear accountability.
- Best TPM-PM pairs share metrics and challenge each other constructively.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. How do you resolve a conflict between PM and engineering on scope?
2. How do you bring technical constraints into product decisions?
3. Describe a time you pushed back on a PM's prioritization.
4. How do you keep PM and TPM aligned on success metrics?
5. What makes a TPM-PM partnership dysfunctional?

## Key Takeaways
- TPM-PM role clarity prevents friction and duplication.
- TPMs add most value at the execution-product interface.
- The best partnerships share outcomes, not just work.
