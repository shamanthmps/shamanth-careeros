# Conflict Stories

## Executive Summary
Conflict stories test your ability to work through disagreements constructively — with peers, senior stakeholders, or teams. Interviewers are not looking for stories where you avoided conflict. They are looking for stories where you leaned in and produced a better outcome.

Conflict avoidance is a red flag at senior levels.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: TPMs sit at the intersection of competing priorities — conflict is unavoidable.
- Interview relevance: Conflict stories appear in every behavioral round under multiple LP lenses.

## What Makes a Conflict Story Strong
- You leaned in rather than escalated or deferred.
- You distinguished positions from interests.
- You found resolution without damaging the relationship permanently.
- The outcome was better than it would have been without the conflict.

## Conflict Story Categories
- Interpersonal: Friction with a colleague or peer lead.
- Priority conflict: Your program vs another team's priorities.
- Technical conflict: Architecture disagreement with a senior engineer.
- Stakeholder conflict: A PM or executive pushing for something you believed was wrong.
- Cross-team conflict: Two teams on your program that could not agree.

## Conflict Story Framework
Situation: What was the conflict and why did it matter?
Task: What was your role — were you a party or a mediator?
Action: How did you surface the conflict, understand underlying interests, and work toward resolution?
Result: What was the outcome for the program AND the relationship?

## Example Story Frame
An engineering lead and a security lead were in a deadlock over a data handling approach, blocking a critical feature release for 11 days. I facilitated a structured trade-off session, mapping each team's concerns to specific measurable criteria. I asked both leads to agree on criteria before defending their position. Within 90 minutes they had agreed on a hybrid approach neither had proposed initially. The feature launched the following week. Both leads told me afterward it was the most productive disagreement they had been in.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a peer or stakeholder.
2. Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager.
3. Describe a time two teams on your program could not align. What did you do?
4. How do you handle conflict with someone more senior than you?
5. Tell me about a time you had to deliver feedback someone did not want to hear.

## Key Takeaways
- Leaning in beats avoiding. Conflict that is addressed early is resolved cheaply.
- Interests over positions is the most reliable resolution framework.
- Relationship outcome matters as much as program outcome.
