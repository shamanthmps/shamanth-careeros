# Google Behavioral Interviews

## Executive Summary
Google evaluates candidates on four core attributes across all rounds: General Cognitive Ability, Leadership, Googleyness, and Role-Related Knowledge. Behavioral questions at Google are structured to gather evidence for these attributes — not to check LP boxes.

Understanding Google's framework helps you calibrate your stories to what their interviewers are actually scoring.

## Google's Four Hiring Attributes

| Attribute | What Interviewers Are Assessing |
|---|---|
| General Cognitive Ability | How you approach problems, structure thinking, and learn |
| Leadership | How you influence and mobilize others without necessarily managing them |
| Googleyness | Authenticity, comfort with ambiguity, humility, collaborative drive |
| Role-Related Knowledge | Demonstrated capability and depth relevant to the role |

## How Google's Behavioral Questions Differ
- Less formulaic than Amazon LP questions.
- More open-ended: "Tell me about a time you had a difficult technical decision."
- Interviewers probe depth with follow-ups: "Why did you do X? What else did you consider?"
- Googleyness is evaluated through how you engage, not just what you say.

## Googleyness Signals
Positive signals:
- Genuine enthusiasm for the problem domain.
- Comfort saying "I don't know — here's how I would find out."
- Collaborative framing that gives others credit appropriately.
- Intellectual honesty about trade-offs and uncertainty.

Negative signals:
- Trying too hard to sound impressive.
- Claiming certainty you do not have.
- Positioning yourself as uniquely responsible for all outcomes.

## Top Google TPM Behavioral Question Patterns
- "Tell me about a cross-functional program where you had to navigate significant ambiguity."
- "Describe a time you had to lead through a major organizational change."
- "How do you handle disagreement with a technical decision made by a senior engineer?"
- "Tell me about a time you simplified a complex process."
- "How do you approach a new domain you have no experience in?"

## Calibration Tips for Google
- Use "I" but give appropriate credit: Google values collaboration, not lone heroism.
- Show thinking process, not just outcome: "Here is how I approached it..." scores cognitive ability.
- Demonstrate learning: Google values candidates who grow, not just those who perform.
- Be authentic: Googleyness cannot be performed — interviewers sense overscripted answers.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you drove a program outcome under extreme ambiguity.
2. How do you handle a situation where your technical recommendation is overruled?
3. Tell me about a time you had to influence an organization through culture change.
4. Describe a time you had to quickly develop expertise in a new technical domain.
5. How do you decide when to push for a decision vs when to keep gathering data?

## Key Takeaways
- Google evaluates thinking process as much as outcomes.
- Googleyness means authentic, humble, collaborative, and intellectually honest.
- Stories that show learning, growth, and genuine reflection score well.
