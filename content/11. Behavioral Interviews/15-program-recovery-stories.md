# Program Recovery Stories

## Executive Summary
Program recovery stories demonstrate that you can take a failing program, diagnose what is wrong, and create a credible path forward — while managing stakeholders honestly. Every experienced TPM has had a program in distress. What you did in that moment reveals your true caliber.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Recovery programs are often the highest-profile and highest-risk situations a TPM faces.
- Interview relevance: "Tell me about a program that was in trouble" is a top-5 TPM interview question.

## What Recovery Stories Reveal
- Your ability to diagnose: What actually was wrong, not just symptoms?
- Your courage: Did you surface the problem honestly or hide it?
- Your execution: What specific actions changed the trajectory?
- Your stakeholder management: How did you communicate during the recovery?

## Recovery Story Framework
Situation: What was the program's state when you engaged? What were the signals of distress?
Task: What was your role — did you inherit it, get parachuted in, or self-identify the problem?
Action: How did you diagnose the root causes? What changes did you make? How did you communicate?
Result: What was the outcome? Did the program fully recover? What did you learn about program health?

## Root Cause Categories for Recovery
- Unclear ownership or accountability gaps.
- Missed dependencies that were not tracked.
- Scope growth without resource adjustment.
- Team morale and motivation breakdown.
- Stakeholder trust erosion from communication failures.

## Example Story Frame
I inherited a platform migration that was 10 weeks behind with no credible revised plan. In my first week I ran individual interviews with all 8 team leads and mapped the actual dependency chain vs the planned one. I found 3 root causes: two untracked external dependencies, a scope change that added 6 weeks of work with no resource adjustment, and a weekly sync that produced no decisions. I rebuilt the plan, removed the untracked dependencies from scope for v1, secured 2 additional engineers, and replaced the weekly sync with a 20-minute decision meeting. We delivered the recovery version 4 weeks later. It was the right scope. The two removed dependencies became v2 in the next quarter.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a program that was failing and how you turned it around.
2. How do you diagnose why a program is in distress?
3. How do you communicate a program in trouble to executive stakeholders?
4. Tell me about a time you had to reset scope to save a program.
5. What early warning signs do you now look for based on recovery experiences?

## Key Takeaways
- Honest diagnosis beats optimistic spin. Interviewers know programs fail.
- Root cause thinking is the signal — not just action.
- Stakeholder communication during recovery is often the hardest and most important skill.
