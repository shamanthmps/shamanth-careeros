# Innovation Stories

## Executive Summary
Innovation stories demonstrate your ability to invent, simplify, or find novel solutions — not necessarily through breakthrough ideas, but through persistent creative problem-solving. At Amazon this maps to "Invent and Simplify." At Google it maps to "Being Googlely" and thinking big.

The bar is not having invented something remarkable. The bar is showing you think beyond the obvious.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: TPMs who bring creative approaches to coordination, process, and product problems create leverage.
- Interview relevance: Tested in all MAANG+ LP frameworks explicitly or implicitly.

## What Makes an Innovation Story Credible
- You identified a problem others accepted as unavoidable.
- Your solution was simpler or faster than the obvious approach.
- You experimented and iterated rather than designing the perfect solution upfront.
- Others adopted or scaled your approach.

## Innovation Story Framework
Situation: What was the status quo that was inefficient or broken?
Task: What were you trying to improve or create?
Action: What novel approach did you take? Why was it different from the obvious path?
Result: What changed? Did others adopt it? Did it scale?

## Example Story Frame
Our cross-team dependency tracking process required 4 different Jira boards, 3 manually updated spreadsheets, and a weekly 90-minute sync just to know what was blocked. I replaced it with a single dependency register template, a 20-minute weekly async update model, and a biweekly decision meeting for only the items that were genuinely stuck. Engineering time spent on coordination dropped by 60%. Six other programs adopted the model within 2 quarters.

## Subtypes of Innovation Stories
- Process simplification: Eliminated unnecessary steps.
- Technical workaround: Found a creative path around a constraint.
- Tool creation: Built something lightweight that filled a gap.
- Reframing: Changed how the team thought about the problem entirely.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you invented a new approach to a persistent problem.
2. Describe a time you simplified something that was overly complex.
3. Tell me about a creative solution you developed under pressure.
4. How do you generate ideas when facing a hard constraint?
5. Tell me about a process improvement you created that others adopted.

## Key Takeaways
- Innovation for TPMs is usually simplification and adaptation, not invention.
- Adoption by others validates the quality of the innovation.
- Experimentation signals are valued: you tried, learned, adjusted.
