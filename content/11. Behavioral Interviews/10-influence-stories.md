# Influence Stories

## Executive Summary
Influence stories show that you can move people, programs, and organizations without formal authority. This is the highest-leverage behavioral category for TPMs and the one most directly tied to program outcomes.

Every senior TPM answer ultimately comes back to: how did you get people who did not have to listen to you to act on your priorities?

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Almost all TPM outcomes require influencing people outside your reporting line.
- Interview relevance: Influence appears under LP, leadership, and cross-functional categories at all companies.

## Influence Story Archetypes
- Influenced a senior stakeholder to change a decision.
- Got engineering teams to prioritize your work over competing demands.
- Changed organizational direction through a document, proposal, or framework.
- Turned a skeptic into a supporter without escalating.

## Influence Story Framework
Situation: Who needed to be influenced? Why did they not have to listen to you?
Task: What outcome required their alignment?
Action: How did you understand their perspective? How did you frame your request? What did you trade?
Result: What changed because of your influence? What was the business impact?

## Key Techniques to Reference
- Framing to their interests: "This protects you from X" not just "this is good for the program."
- Data over opinion: Evidence reduces defensiveness.
- Private before public: Difficult influence conversations work better 1:1.
- Earned credibility: Past reliability creates future influence.

## Example Story Frame
An infra team lead was resistant to prioritizing a platform migration that my program depended on. I asked for 30 minutes to understand their concerns — they had three migrations already competing for their team's attention. I worked with their PM to show how my migration would reduce their ongoing maintenance burden by 20%. I also offered to absorb the coordination overhead. The lead agreed to start in 6 weeks. The migration was the first one their team delivered ahead of schedule.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you influenced a decision without authority.
2. Describe a time you convinced a skeptical senior leader.
3. Tell me about a time you changed organizational direction through influence.
4. How do you get engineering teams to prioritize your work when they have competing demands?
5. Tell me about a time your influence failed and what you learned.

## Key Takeaways
- Influence is built over time, not deployed in the moment.
- Understanding what the other party needs is the first step.
- The best influence stories are ones where the other party felt they gained something.
