# Customer Obsession Stories

## Executive Summary
Customer obsession stories demonstrate that you start decisions from user needs, not internal convenience. At Amazon, this is LP #1. At Google and Meta, it underpins most product and leadership evaluations. The strongest candidates have concrete examples of advocating for users in the face of competing internal pressure.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Programs without customer grounding can optimize delivery of the wrong outcome.
- Interview relevance: Amazon scores this explicitly. All MAANG+ companies value it implicitly.

## What Customer Obsession Looks Like in Interviews
- You had data or insight about user needs that others did not have or were ignoring.
- You challenged a roadmap item, technical decision, or timeline because of user impact.
- You pushed for a better user outcome when the business pressure was in the other direction.
- You used working backward to define success before starting delivery.

## Customer Obsession Story Framework
Situation: What decision or direction was being pursued?
Task: What did you know or believe about the customer that made you act?
Action: How did you advocate for the customer perspective? What did you do?
Result: What changed? What was the customer impact?

## Example Story Frame
Our platform team was planning to ship a new authentication flow that reduced developer overhead. I reviewed the support ticket data and saw that 40% of our highest-value enterprise users were already struggling with the existing auth flow. Shipping a new one would have doubled their remediation work mid-program. I brought the support data to the PM and engineering lead and proposed a parallel migration path for enterprise users. It added 2 weeks to the timeline but preserved trust with our highest-LTV segment. The PM later told me it was the most important scope challenge of the quarter.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you advocated for customers over internal convenience.
2. Describe a time you used working backward to define a program's success.
3. How do you keep teams connected to customer impact during long programs?
4. Tell me about a decision you challenged because it would hurt users.
5. How do you represent customer needs when customers are not in the room?

## Key Takeaways
- Customer obsession is an operational behavior, evidenced in decisions, not stated as a value.
- The strongest stories show customer advocacy against internal pressure.
- Working backward is a concrete practice, not just a phrase.
