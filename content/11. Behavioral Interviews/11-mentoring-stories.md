# Mentoring Stories

## Executive Summary
Mentoring stories demonstrate that you invest in others' growth and that your leadership multiplies beyond your immediate work. At Senior and Staff levels, interviewers expect evidence that your impact extends to people development — not just program outcomes.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: TPMs who develop others build organizational trust and capability.
- Interview relevance: People development is scored in leadership and bar-raiser rounds at all MAANG+ companies.

## What Strong Mentoring Stories Show
- You identified a specific gap or opportunity in someone else's development.
- You took deliberate action beyond your assigned responsibilities.
- The person grew measurably — in skill, confidence, or career.
- You sponsored, not just advised.

## Mentoring vs Sponsoring
Mentoring: You gave advice, guidance, and feedback.
Sponsoring: You created opportunities, advocated in rooms they were not in, or put your own credibility on the line for them.
The strongest stories include both.

## Mentoring Story Framework
Situation: Who were you developing and what was their starting point?
Task: What gap or growth goal were you working on with them?
Action: What specific things did you do — questions, feedback, opportunities, advocacy?
Result: What did they achieve? What changed in their capability or trajectory?

## Example Story Frame
A junior TPM on my program was technically strong but struggled to communicate upward — she buried the key message in context. I worked with her for 6 weeks: first I gave her specific feedback after each stakeholder communication, then I coached her to prepare the 30-second version before entering any VP meeting, then I created an opportunity for her to run the monthly program review independently. Her next 360 feedback cited executive communication as a strength. She was promoted to Senior TPM within 9 months and told me directly that our work together was the turning point.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about someone you mentored and what you did.
2. How do you develop people who do not report to you?
3. Tell me about a time you sponsored someone's advancement.
4. How do you give developmental feedback to someone who is resistant?
5. What is the most important thing you have done for someone else's career?

## Key Takeaways
- Mentoring stories signal organizational investment and senior maturity.
- Sponsorship amplifies mentoring — advocacy in rooms they cannot access.
- The outcome is their growth, not your effort.
