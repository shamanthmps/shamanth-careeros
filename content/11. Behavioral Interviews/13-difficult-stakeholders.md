# Difficult Stakeholders

## Executive Summary
Difficult stakeholder stories demonstrate that you can maintain relationships and drive outcomes even with stakeholders who are skeptical, adversarial, absent, or unpredictable. Every TPM has these stories. The best candidates show nuanced handling — not complaint, not avoidance, not escalation as a first move.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: One difficult stakeholder can derail a program. Managing them well is a core skill.
- Interview relevance: Every MAANG+ behavioral round will probe cross-functional relationship management.

## Difficult Stakeholder Archetypes
- The skeptic: Does not believe the program will succeed or is worth the investment.
- The blocker: Has authority and consistently withholds it.
- The scope creep driver: Keeps adding requirements that destabilize the program.
- The absent stakeholder: Needed for decisions but perpetually unavailable.
- The political actor: Pursues their own agenda at the expense of program success.

## Story Framework
Situation: Who was the difficult stakeholder and why did they matter to the program?
Task: What outcome required their engagement or alignment?
Action: How did you understand their behavior, adapt your approach, and work through it?
Result: What changed — in the relationship, the stakeholder's engagement, or the program outcome?

## Key Principles
- Understand before judging: Stakeholder behavior is usually rational from their perspective.
- Address privately first: Public pressure creates defensiveness.
- Escalate as a last resort with clear evidence: Not as pressure, but as information.

## Example Story Frame
A finance stakeholder was consistently late to review meetings and non-committal in approvals, creating 2-week delays per cycle. I requested a 1:1 to understand the situation. I discovered they were in 3 concurrent budget cycles with competing approvals. I adapted: I created a 1-page pre-read that arrived 5 business days early, reduced meeting ask to 20 minutes and 2 explicit decisions, and offered to handle all follow-up actions. Approval cycle time dropped from 2 weeks to 3 days. They became a program champion in the executive review.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you worked with a very difficult stakeholder.
2. How do you handle a stakeholder who consistently misses commitments?
3. Describe a time a stakeholder was trying to undermine your program.
4. How do you manage a senior stakeholder who keeps expanding scope?
5. What do you do when a critical stakeholder goes completely unresponsive?

## Key Takeaways
- Difficult stakeholder behavior usually has an understandable root cause.
- Adapt the interaction model before escalating.
- The outcome metric is program delivery AND relationship preservation.
