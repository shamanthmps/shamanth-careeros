# Executive Storytelling

## Executive Summary
Executive storytelling is the ability to communicate complex technical and program situations in narratives that are clear, credible, and actionable for senior audiences. It is one of the highest-leverage communication skills for TPMs.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Executives trust TPMs who can make complex programs understandable.
- Interview relevance: Every answer you give in an interview is a story — and it is evaluated as one.

## Core Concepts
### Story Structures
- Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR): Context, conflict, outcome.
- BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front — lead with the point, then explain.
- Problem-Solution-Impact: What was wrong, what you did, what changed.

### Internal Working
Know your audience -> identify the one key message -> lead with it -> provide just enough context -> make the ask or offer the option.

### ASCII Diagram
Audience + Purpose -> Lead Message -> Supporting Context -> Call to Action

### Trade-offs
- Too much detail loses executives.
- Too little detail loses credibility.

## Real-world Example
Before a critical executive review, a TPM rewrote the program narrative from: "Here is a timeline with milestones..." to: "We are on track. One decision we need from you today is X, which has a deadline of Friday." The meeting took 15 minutes instead of 45.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- What is the single thing I need this person to understand or decide?
- What background is genuinely necessary vs just comfortable for me to include?
- Am I inviting the audience into the story or lecturing them?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I structure all executive communication as BLUF — conclusion first, then supporting evidence. I prepare the 30-second version of every message before entering any executive conversation and expand only if they ask. This respects their time and demonstrates I know what I'm talking about."

## Common Mistakes
- Starting with background instead of conclusion.
- Using jargon that signals depth but loses the audience.
- Ending without a clear ask or next step.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Lead with conclusion, follow with context.
- One key message per communication.
- Make the ask clear before leaving the room.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. How do you communicate a major risk to a VP who is already stressed?
2. Tell me about a time your executive communication changed the outcome.
3. How do you prepare for a 10-minute executive review?
4. How do you handle an executive who keeps derailing with questions?
5. What is your framework for written executive updates?

## Key Takeaways
- Executive storytelling is a craft that can be practiced.
- BLUF and SCR are reliable frameworks.
- Interviewers evaluate your storytelling in every answer you give.
