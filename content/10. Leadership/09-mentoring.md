# Mentoring

## Executive Summary
Mentoring is sharing your experience and perspective to help someone navigate their career or grow their skills. For senior TPMs, being a visible mentor is both a leadership obligation and an interview signal of organizational investment.

Interviewers evaluate mentoring through examples of how you helped others grow beyond immediate program needs.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Mentoring builds organizational trust and talent pipeline.
- Interview relevance: Expected evidence of leadership impact beyond program execution.

## Core Concepts
### Definitions
- Mentor: Experienced guide sharing perspective and network access.
- Sponsorship: Active advocacy for someone's advancement.
- Developmental conversation: Structured discussion about growth goals and paths.

### Internal Working
Establish relationship -> understand mentee's goals -> share relevant experience -> open networks -> support specific transitions.

### ASCII Diagram
Relationship -> Goals -> Experience Sharing -> Network -> Growth

### Trade-offs
- Depth of mentorship vs time available.
- Advice from your experience vs leaving space for mentee to discover.

## Real-world Example
A junior TPM wanted to move into Staff level but lacked visibility. Their mentor created opportunities for them to present to VP-level audiences, sponsored them for a high-profile program, and gave them specific feedback on communication style. They were promoted within 18 months.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- What does this person actually want for their career — not what I would want?
- Where can I open doors that they cannot open themselves?
- Am I advising based on my path or helping them navigate theirs?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I mentor by investing in understanding what the person wants and then helping connect their strengths to opportunities. I also sponsor actively — mentorship without sponsorship can leave people stuck despite development."

## Common Mistakes
- Mentoring only people similar to yourself.
- Giving advice without listening to what the person actually wants.
- Mentoring without ever sponsoring.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Mentoring = experience sharing + network + sponsorship.
- Sponsorship (advocacy) amplifies mentorship.
- Effective mentors listen before they advise.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about someone you mentored. What was your approach?
2. How do you mentor someone whose situation is very different from your own experience?
3. What is the difference between a mentor and a sponsor?
4. How do you handle a mentee who does not take your advice?
5. How do you find time for mentoring amid heavy program responsibilities?

## Key Takeaways
- Mentoring is a leadership multiplier with career-level impact.
- Sponsorship converts mentoring into advancement.
- TPMs who mentor create organizational goodwill and talent pipeline.
