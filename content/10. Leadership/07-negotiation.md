# Negotiation

## Executive Summary
Negotiation for TPMs is not adversarial bargaining — it is principled alignment that produces durable commitments. Every scope conversation, resourcing decision, and timeline discussion is a negotiation. Senior candidates show they can negotiate with credibility and without damaging relationships.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Resource and timeline negotiations are constant in program management.
- Interview relevance: Embedded in many influence and conflict behavioral questions.

## Core Concepts
### Definitions
- BATNA: Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — your leverage.
- Anchoring: Setting the first number to influence the range of discussion.
- Principled negotiation: Focus on interests and objective criteria, not positions.

### Internal Working
Identify interests -> know your BATNA -> anchor appropriately -> trade on differences -> close with clear commitment.

### ASCII Diagram
My Interests + Their Interests -> Shared Criteria -> Trade-offs -> Committed Agreement

### Trade-offs
- Winning a negotiation aggressively but damaging long-term relationship.
- Compromising too quickly to preserve relationship.

## Real-world Example
A TPM needed 3 engineers from a team whose manager was protective of resources. Instead of asking for headcount, they negotiated a 6-week focused engagement with a defined scope and clear exit criteria. The manager agreed immediately because the ask felt bounded.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- What does the other party actually need, underneath their stated position?
- What can I trade that costs me little but is valuable to them?
- What is my BATNA if this negotiation fails?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I negotiate on interests, not positions. I prepare by understanding what matters to the other party, identify what I can offer in exchange, and anchor the conversation on shared success criteria rather than competing demands."

## Common Mistakes
- Negotiating without knowing your BATNA.
- Focusing on position rather than underlying interest.
- Treating all negotiations as zero-sum.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Principled negotiation = interests + criteria, not positions.
- Know your BATNA before every significant negotiation.
- Durable agreements require both parties to feel they gained.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a difficult resource negotiation.
2. How do you negotiate a timeline with an executive stakeholder?
3. What do you do when a negotiation stalls?
4. How do you maintain relationships after a tough negotiation?
5. When do you walk away from a negotiation?

## Key Takeaways
- Negotiation is a core TPM skill — it happens constantly.
- Principled approaches produce better and more durable outcomes.
- Relationship preservation is a constraint, not a weakness.
