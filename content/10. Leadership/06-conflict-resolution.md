# Conflict Resolution

## Executive Summary
Conflict is inevitable in cross-functional programs. The ability to surface, address, and resolve conflict productively is a core TPM leadership skill. Interviewers distinguish candidates who manage conflict from those who avoid it.

Strong answers show you lean into conflict with structured approaches rather than escalating or deferring.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Unresolved conflict stalls programs and erodes team trust.
- Interview relevance: Conflict resolution examples are expected in behavioral rounds.

## Core Concepts
### Conflict Types
- Interpersonal conflict: Relationship friction between individuals.
- Priority conflict: Competing demands on shared resources.
- Technical conflict: Disagreement on approach or architecture.
- Organizational conflict: Structural tension between teams or functions.

### Internal Working
Identify conflict type -> understand underlying interests -> create safe resolution space -> facilitate to decision -> repair relationships if needed.

### ASCII Diagram
Conflict Detected -> Underlying Interests -> Safe Space -> Resolution -> Repair

### Trade-offs
- Addressing conflict early takes courage but prevents compound damage.
- Delayed resolution feels easier but compounds costs.

## Real-world Example
Two engineering leads disagreed on a database migration approach, blocking a release for two weeks. The TPM facilitated a structured trade-off meeting, documented both options with explicit criteria, and drove to a decision with a 4-week review checkpoint. The conflict was resolved in one session.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- What are the underlying interests behind each position?
- Is this a data conflict (both sides have different facts) or values conflict (different criteria)?
- Who needs to be in the room and who should not?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I distinguish between the position people hold and the underlying interest driving it. When I facilitate conflict resolution, I focus teams on shared criteria rather than defending positions, which usually reveals more common ground than expected."

## Common Mistakes
- Avoiding conflict until it becomes a crisis.
- Taking sides in interpersonal conflicts.
- Resolving symptom without addressing root cause.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Conflict = positions vs interests distinction.
- Facilitate, do not arbitrate, when possible.
- Repair relationships after resolution.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a conflict between two teams on your program. How did you resolve it?
2. How do you handle a conflict where you are also a party?
3. When do you escalate vs resolve directly?
4. How do you distinguish a technical disagreement from an interpersonal one?
5. How do you prevent the same conflict recurring?

## Key Takeaways
- Productive conflict resolution is a senior leadership skill.
- Interests over positions is the most reliable framework.
- TPMs who lean into conflict earn trust; those who avoid it lose credibility.
