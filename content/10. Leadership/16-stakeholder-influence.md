# Stakeholder Influence

## Executive Summary
Stakeholder influence is the ability to shape beliefs, decisions, and commitments across people with different goals, levels, and functions. For TPMs, stakeholders include executives, engineers, PMs, legal, finance, customers, and partners — often simultaneously.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: All program success runs through stakeholder alignment.
- Interview relevance: Tested across multiple behavioral categories.

## Core Concepts
### Stakeholder Map Dimensions
- Power: Decision-making authority.
- Interest: Stake in the program outcome.
- Attitude: Current disposition toward the program (supportive, neutral, resistant).

### Internal Working
Map stakeholders -> understand their goals and concerns -> tailor engagement frequency and message -> maintain relationships proactively.

### ASCII Diagram
Power + Interest + Attitude -> Engagement Strategy -> Trust -> Influence

### Trade-offs
- Broad stakeholder engagement takes time but prevents late surprises.
- Narrow engagement is faster but creates blind spots.

## Real-world Example
A TPM identified that a finance stakeholder was silently skeptical of a major infrastructure program. Rather than waiting for review objections, they scheduled a private conversation, understood the cost concerns, incorporated them into the business case, and turned the stakeholder into a program sponsor.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- Who are the stakeholders I am under-investing in?
- What does each stakeholder need to say yes — and what are they afraid of?
- Am I communicating at the right frequency for each stakeholder tier?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I map stakeholders by power, interest, and current attitude, then invest engagement proportionally. I focus on skeptics before they become blockers and treat every stakeholder interaction as an opportunity to build trust, not just share status."

## Common Mistakes
- Only engaging stakeholders at formal review milestones.
- Treating all stakeholders with the same message.
- Ignoring silent stakeholders until they object publicly.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Map stakeholders before programs start.
- Engage skeptics early and privately.
- Tailor message to level, function, and concern.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. How do you handle a senior stakeholder who is silently resistant to your program?
2. How do you maintain alignment across 15+ stakeholders?
3. Tell me about a time you turned a skeptic into a supporter.
4. How do you communicate with executives differently than with ICs?
5. What do you do when a key stakeholder goes missing mid-program?

## Key Takeaways
- Stakeholder influence is proactive, not reactive.
- Silent skeptics are higher risk than vocal opponents.
- TPMs who manage stakeholders well experience fewer late surprises.
