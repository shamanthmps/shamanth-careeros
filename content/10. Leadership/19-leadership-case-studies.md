# Leadership Case Studies

## Executive Summary
This lesson synthesizes leadership topics through three scenarios drawn from real TPM interview patterns. Use these as practice templates — not for memorization, but to sharpen your STAR structure and behavioral recall.

## Case Study 1: Turning Around a Struggling Cross-functional Program

Situation: A 9-team program with a critical Q3 deadline was 6 weeks behind with no clear owner of the coordination problem. Multiple teams pointed at each other for missed handoffs.

Approach:
- Mapped the dependency chain and identified three critical blockers.
- Owned the coordination layer without taking over the individual team leads.
- Created a weekly escalation forum with engineering VPs from each team.
- Introduced a shared tracker with red/amber/green status visible to all.

Outcome: Program delivered 2 weeks late but with full scope. No stakeholder surprises. Three teams adopted the coordination model for future programs.

Leadership signals demonstrated: ownership, conflict resolution, stakeholder alignment, decision facilitation.

## Case Study 2: Influencing a Decision You Disagreed With

Situation: A senior product leader wanted to ship a feature with known security gaps to meet a marketing deadline.

Approach:
- Prepared a concise risk brief showing business impact of a potential breach.
- Scheduled a private conversation with the PM first to understand their constraints.
- Proposed a compromise: ship to 5% of users with monitoring and delayed full rollout.
- Escalated the risk clearly to the VP while positioning it as a decision to be made, not a crisis to manage.

Outcome: The partial rollout was approved. Monitoring caught edge cases that were fixed before full rollout. The PM credited the TPM with protecting them from a potential incident.

Leadership signals: strategic thinking, difficult conversation, influence without authority, executive presence.

## Case Study 3: Building Trust in a New Organization

Situation: New TPM joining a platform team with a reputation for over-promising and under-delivering. Engineers were skeptical of program management entirely.

Approach:
- Spent first 4 weeks listening and observing — no new processes introduced.
- Identified three specific pain points engineers raised consistently.
- Fixed two small but visible friction points immediately.
- In the first planning cycle, introduced one process change with clear rationale and asked for 4-week trial.

Outcome: Team buy-in built through demonstrated listening and early delivery on commitments. Within 3 months the team requested process improvements rather than resisting them.

Leadership signals: relationship building, change leadership, influence, team motivation.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. Tell me about a time you turned around a failing program.
2. How have you handled a situation where you disagreed with a senior decision?
3. How did you build trust in a new organization or team?
4. Tell me about a time you led without any formal authority.
5. Describe a program where your leadership had the most organizational impact.

## Key Takeaways
- Case studies are the delivery vehicle for your leadership principles.
- Interviewers evaluate process and judgment, not just outcome.
- Your strongest examples should demonstrate multiple leadership behaviors simultaneously.
