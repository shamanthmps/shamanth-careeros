# Team Motivation

## Executive Summary
Team motivation is about creating conditions where engineers, PMs, and cross-functional partners consistently bring discretionary energy to the work. TPMs do not manage most of the people they work with, which makes motivation through environment and meaning rather than reward systems critical.

## Why This Matters
- TPM relevance: Motivated teams ship better work faster and flag problems earlier.
- Interview relevance: Team morale and culture questions appear in senior leadership rounds.

## Core Concepts
### Motivation Drivers
- Autonomy: Freedom to choose how to solve problems.
- Mastery: Opportunity to grow skills that matter.
- Purpose: Connection to meaningful outcomes.
- Progress: Visible advancement toward goals.
- Recognition: Timely, specific appreciation.

### Internal Working
Understand team's motivation landscape -> create conditions for autonomy/mastery/purpose -> celebrate progress -> address blockers.

### ASCII Diagram
Autonomy + Mastery + Purpose + Progress + Recognition -> Discretionary Energy

### Trade-offs
- Intrinsic motivation is more durable but slower to build.
- Extrinsic rewards work short-term but erode intrinsic drive.

## Real-world Example
A TPM noticed the team's energy was low midway through a long migration. They created a visible "done" board, celebrated weekly milestones, and organized a demo showing the product impact of their technical work. Engagement visibly improved within two weeks.

## TPM Perspective
Ask:
- Does this team understand why their work matters?
- Are there blockers making work unnecessarily frustrating?
- When did I last recognize someone specifically and publicly?

## Interview Perspective
Strong answer:
"I motivate by making purpose visible, removing friction, and creating visible progress markers. For teams I do not manage, I invest in recognition, advocate for their work in executive forums, and connect their contributions to business outcomes."

## Common Mistakes
- Only motivating through urgency and pressure.
- Generic recognition with no specifics.
- Ignoring team energy signals until morale is already low.

## 5-Minute Revision
- Autonomy + mastery + purpose are more durable than rewards.
- Progress visibility is underrated motivation.
- Specific recognition is more valuable than praise.

## Practice Interview Questions
1. How do you keep a cross-functional team motivated through a long program?
2. Tell me about a time you turned around low team morale.
3. How do you motivate engineers who do not report to you?
4. How do you recognize contributions across a distributed team?
5. How do you communicate purpose to a team doing unglamorous work?

## Key Takeaways
- Motivation through purpose and progress is more effective than pressure.
- TPMs create motivating environments without formal authority.
- Recognition and meaning are leadership acts.
